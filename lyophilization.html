Lyophilization Technology for RPA Enzymes

Room-Temperature Stable Reagents for Point-of-Care & Field Diagnostics

What is Lyophilization?

Freeze-drying is a powerful dehydration technology that preserves biological molecules by removing water under vacuum at low temperatures

Lyophilization (also known as freeze-drying) is a process in which water is removed from a product through sublimation — the direct transition from solid ice to water vapour — without passing through the liquid phase. This gentle dehydration preserves the structure and function of sensitive biological molecules such as proteins, enzymes, and nucleic acids, enabling long-term storage at ambient temperatures.

The Three Phases of Lyophilization

1

Freezing

The product is cooled below its eutectic point (typically -40°C to -80°C), converting all free water into ice crystals. Rapid or controlled freezing determines crystal size and affects downstream drying efficiency.

2

Primary Drying

Under vacuum conditions, ice sublimes directly into vapour. This phase removes ~95% of water content. Temperature and pressure are carefully controlled to maintain the frozen matrix while maximising sublimation rate.

3

Secondary Drying

Bound (adsorbed) water molecules are removed by gradually raising the temperature (typically 20-40°C) under continued vacuum. Residual moisture is reduced to <1-3%, ensuring long-term stability.

Why Lyophilization Matters for Molecular Diagnostics

  • Water Removal — Eliminates the medium for hydrolysis and microbial growth that degrade biological reagents
  • Extended Shelf Life — Transforms reagents from weeks of refrigerated storage to 12+ months at room temperature
  • Room-Temperature Storage — Breaks the cold chain dependency, enabling deployment in resource-limited and field settings
  • Reduced Mass & Volume — Lighter, more compact shipping; no dry ice or refrigerated containers needed

Key Technical Challenges

Dangkang Bio has the expertise and infrastructure to overcome the critical challenges in lyophilizing RPA enzymes

Glycerol-Free Formulation

Traditional enzyme preparations contain glycerol (typically 50% v/v) as a cryoprotectant to prevent ice crystal damage during frozen storage. However, glycerol presents significant problems for lyophilization:

  • High viscosity — impedes accurate pipetting and automated dispensing
  • Collapses the lyophilized cake — glycerol has a low collapse temperature, leading to melted or shrunken product appearance
  • Retains residual moisture — hygroscopic nature hinders achieving target residual water levels
  • Inhibits downstream reactions — residual glycerol can affect RPA amplification efficiency

Dangkang Bio develops glycerol-free enzyme formulations by optimising expression conditions, purification protocols, and storage buffer composition to maintain enzyme stability without glycerol — ensuring excellent lyophilisation performance and full activity recovery.

Cryoprotectant Selection

Choosing the right cryoprotectant (lyoprotectant) is critical for preserving enzyme activity during freeze-drying and storage:

  • Trehalose (D-Trehalose) — The gold standard. Forms a stable glassy matrix (vitrification) that protects protein structure by replacing water hydrogen bonds. Non-reducing sugar prevents Maillard browning reactions.
  • Sucrose — A cost-effective alternative with similar cryoprotective properties. Widely used in combination with trehalose.
  • D-Trehalose vs. L-Trehalose — Only D-trehalose (the naturally occurring diastereomer) is suitable for biopreservation. L-trehalose lacks the correct stereochemistry and must be excluded to ensure purity and performance.
  • Concentration Optimisation — Typically 5-10% (w/v). Too little provides insufficient protection; too much increases viscosity and may reduce enzyme activity per unit volume. Dangkang Bio optimises concentration for each enzyme through systematic screening.

Lyophilization Cycle Optimisation

Each enzyme product requires a custom lyophilization cycle (recipe) to achieve optimal results:

  • Pre-freezing Temperature & Time — Must reach below the product's glass transition temperature (Tg'). Typical range: -40°C to -80°C with controlled ramp rates.
  • Primary Drying — Sublimation Rate Control — Shelf temperature and chamber pressure are balanced to maximise ice removal rate without collapsing the frozen matrix. Typically 24-48 hours.
  • Secondary Drying — Desorption Temperature — Gradually raised to 20-40°C to remove bound water. Duration depends on product load and vial configuration.
  • Residual Moisture Control — Target: <1-3% (w/w). Too high risks chemical/biological degradation; too low may cause protein denaturation from over-drying.

Enzyme Activity Recovery

The ultimate measure of lyophilization success is how much enzymatic activity is retained after the process:

  • Target: ≥90% activity recovery — Post-lyophilization enzyme activity compared to fresh (non-lyophilized) control
  • Multiple freeze-thaw stability testing — Ensures the enzyme tolerates temperature excursions during shipping and use
  • Accelerated stability testing — Storage at elevated temperatures (37°C, 45°C, 55°C) to predict real-time shelf life using the Arrhenius model
  • Real-time stability monitoring — Ongoing testing at 25°C and 37°C to validate shelf life claims

Lyophilization Process Flow

From enzyme expression to final packaging — our end-to-end lyophilized RPA enzyme production workflow

Enzyme Expression & Purification

Recombinant RPA enzyme proteins are expressed in E. coli host systems and purified through affinity chromatography, ion exchange, and size exclusion steps to achieve high purity (>95%).

1

Glycerol Removal / Buffer Exchange

Enzyme solutions undergo diafiltration or desalting column treatment to remove glycerol and exchange into a lyophilization-compatible buffer system (e.g., Tris-HEPES with trehalose).

2

Cryoprotectant Formulation

Lyoprotectant (D-trehalose, sucrose, or combination) is added at optimised concentration. Additional excipients (bulking agents, surfactants, pH buffers) may be included based on the target format.

3

Filling & Pre-Freezing

Enzyme formulation is dispensed into vials or tray wells (for LyoBead format) and frozen to -40°C to -80°C. Controlled freezing rates ensure optimal ice crystal formation.

4

Primary Drying (Sublimation)

Under vacuum, ice sublimes directly to vapour. Shelf temperature and chamber pressure are precisely controlled over 24-48 hours to remove ~95% of water content while preserving product structure.

5

Secondary Drying (Desorption)

Temperature is gradually increased (20-40°C) under continued vacuum to remove bound/adsorbed water molecules. Residual moisture is reduced to the target level of <3%.

6

Quality Control

Rigorous testing including residual moisture content (Karl Fischer), enzyme activity assay, appearance inspection, sterility testing, endotoxin levels, and batch-to-batch consistency (CV <10%).

7

Packaging & Storage

Vials are stoppered and sealed under inert gas (nitrogen/argon) in the lyophilizer chamber or a controlled-atmosphere glovebox. Final products are stored at room temperature pending distribution.
