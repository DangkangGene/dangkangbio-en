RPA Technology

Recombinase Polymerase Amplification - A Revolutionary Isothermal Nucleic Acid Detection Method

What is RPA?

Recombinase Polymerase Amplification (RPA) is a novel isothermal nucleic acid amplification technology that operates at low temperatures (37-42°C). Unlike traditional PCR which requires precise thermal cycling, RPA achieves rapid DNA amplification through the coordinated action of three key proteins.

For suitably designed assays, RPA can produce detectable amplification rapidly at low constant temperature. Analytical sensitivity and time to result remain dependent on target, primer/probe design, sample preparation and readout format.

  • Operates at constant low temperature (37-42°C)
  • Rapid amplification in 5-20 minutes
  • Assay-dependent analytical sensitivity
  • Portable and field-deployable
  • Minimal equipment requirements

How RPA Works

The RPA mechanism involves three core enzymes working in concert

1. Recombinase (UvsX)

The recombinase protein forms a complex with primers and scans the double-stranded DNA template. When it identifies homologous sequences, it mediates strand exchange, displacing the original strand and inserting the primer.

2. Single-Strand Binding Protein (gp32)

SSB proteins bind to the displaced single DNA strand, stabilizing it and preventing reannealing. This keeps the template strand accessible for primer extension and DNA synthesis.

3. Strand-Displacing Polymerase (Bsu)

The DNA polymerase extends the primer from its 3' end, using the template strand as a guide. Its strand-displacement activity synthesizes new DNA while pushing aside the original strand.

Amplification Cycle

The RPA reaction proceeds through repeated cycles of strand exchange, primer extension, and product release. Each cycle doubles the amount of specific DNA target, leading to exponential amplification.

Real-time monitoring can be achieved using fluorescent probes (exo probe or fpg probe), enabling quantitative detection without the need for gel electrophoresis.

RPA-CRISPR Integration

RPA can rapidly increase target abundance, while programmable CRISPR-Cas recognition converts a matching amplicon into a fluorescence or lateral-flow signal. The combined workflow has strong potential, but performance and integration strategy remain target- and matrix-dependent.

  • Cas12a or Cas13 recognition pathways
  • Fluorescence and lateral-flow readouts
  • Two-step, closed-tube and one-pot architectures
  • Target-specific optimisation and controls
