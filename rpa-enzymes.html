Core RPA Enzyme Components

The recombinase polymerase amplification system relies on three essential enzyme components working in concert to achieve rapid, isothermal DNA amplification

UvsX Recombinase

Derived from T4 Bacteriophage

UvsX is a strand exchange protein that forms a helical nucleoprotein filament with primers. This recombinase-oligonucleotide complex then scans double-stranded DNA templates for homologous sequences and catalyses strand invasion, creating a displacement loop (D-loop) structure that enables primer annealing without thermal denaturation.

UvsX requires ATP hydrolysis to drive the strand exchange reaction and functions optimally in the presence of molecular crowding agents such as polyethylene glycol (PEG), which mimic the intracellular environment and enhance recombination efficiency.

Bsu Large Fragment DNA Polymerase

Derived from Bacillus subtilis

The Bsu Large Fragment is a 5'→3' DNA polymerase lacking 5'→3' exonuclease activity, obtained by proteolytic removal of the N-terminal domain of the native Bsu polymerase. This strand-displacing DNA polymerase extends the primer from its 3' hydroxyl end, synthesising the complementary strand while simultaneously displacing the downstream strand.

Its intrinsic strand displacement activity eliminates the need for a separate helicase, distinguishing RPA from other isothermal amplification methods such as LAMP or HDA. The enzyme operates efficiently at the mild reaction temperatures characteristic of RPA.

T4 Gene 32 Protein (gp32) — SSB

Derived from T4 Bacteriophage

The T4 Gene 32 protein functions as a single-stranded DNA binding protein (SSB). Its primary role in the RPA system is to stabilise the displaced single-stranded DNA strand generated during strand invasion by the recombinase-primer complex.

By coating and protecting the displaced strand, gp32 prevents re-annealing of the two complementary DNA strands and blocks branch migration from reversing the D-loop structure. This ensures the primer remains annealed at the target site and is not expelled, thereby facilitating efficient polymerase extension and exponential amplification.

RPA Reaction Mechanism

The molecular mechanism of recombinase polymerase amplification proceeds through a coordinated series of enzymatic steps at a constant temperature

Recombinase–Primer Complex Formation

UvsX recombinase binds to the oligonucleotide primer in the presence of ATP, forming a right-handed helical nucleoprotein filament. This active complex is essential for subsequent homology search and strand invasion. The nucleoprotein filament protects the primer from degradation and presents it in a conformation suitable for DNA duplex interrogation.

Homology Search in Double-Stranded DNA

The recombinase–primer nucleoprotein filament scans the double-stranded DNA template for a homologous sequence matching the primer. This search process is facilitated by the dynamic nature of the filament, which samples local DNA regions through transient opening and re-closing of the duplex, guided by complementary base-pairing potential.

Strand Invasion & D-Loop Formation

Upon identifying the target homologous site, the recombinase drives the primer strand to invade the double helix, displacing one strand of the duplex and forming a displacement loop (D-loop). The displaced strand is extruded from the duplex structure, creating a single-stranded region available for stabilisation by SSB. This step replaces the thermal denaturation required in PCR.

SSB Stabilisation of the Displaced Strand

T4 Gene 32 protein (gp32) rapidly binds to and coats the displaced single-stranded DNA, preventing it from re-annealing with the complementary strand. This stabilisation also prevents branch migration from reversing the D-loop and expelling the primer. Without gp32, the invasion intermediate would be transient and unproductive.

Polymerase Extension

Bsu Large Fragment DNA polymerase recognises the annealed primer at its 3' end within the D-loop structure and initiates DNA synthesis in the 5'→3' direction. As the polymerase extends the primer, its intrinsic strand displacement activity simultaneously displaces the downstream DNA strand, which is immediately bound by SSB (gp32). The full-length amplicon is generated in a single extension step.

Exponential Amplification Cycle

The newly synthesised strand, freed from the template upon completion of extension, can itself serve as a template for subsequent rounds of recombinase-mediated invasion. Forward and reverse primers operate simultaneously, and with each cycle the number of target amplicon molecules doubles. Combined with the continuous nature of isothermal amplification, this results in exponential accumulation of product within 5–20 minutes.

Detection Strategies

RPA amplicons can be detected through multiple complementary strategies, each suited to different application scenarios and resource settings

Real-time Fluorescence Detection

Uses Exo probes (exonuclease-sensitive dual-labelled probes) for quantitative, real-time monitoring of amplification. The probe is cleaved by the 5'→3' exonuclease activity during extension, separating the fluorophore from the quencher. Enables kinetic analysis and threshold-based quantification with sensitivity down to single-copy detection.

Lateral Flow Assay (LFA)

Employs Nfo probes (nicking endonuclease-sensitive probes) with biotin and FAM labels. After RPA amplification, the product is cleaved by Nt.BstNBI nicking enzyme, and the labelled fragments are visualised on a lateral flow strip within minutes. Ideal for point-of-care, resource-limited settings with no instrumentation required.

Gel Electrophoresis

Standard agarose gel electrophoresis provides a straightforward, widely accessible method for confirming RPA amplicon presence and approximate size. While not real-time, it offers reliable endpoint analysis and is suitable for laboratories with existing electrophoresis infrastructure. Band intensity correlates with amplification efficiency.

CRISPR-Cas Integration

Combining RPA with CRISPR-Cas systems (Cas12a, Cas13a, Cas9) achieves ultra-sensitive, sequence-specific detection. The RPA amplicon activates the Cas effector nuclease, which then cleaves a fluorescent reporter molecule. This RPA-CRISPR cascade delivers attomolar sensitivity and single-base specificity, enabling multiplexed pathogen identification.

Application Fields

RPA technology is transforming nucleic acid detection across diverse fields, from clinical diagnostics to food safety and environmental monitoring

Infectious Disease Diagnostics

Rapid detection of HIV, HPV, SARS-CoV-2, HCMV, and other clinically significant pathogens. RPA enables same-visit results in emergency departments, rural clinics, and low-resource settings where PCR infrastructure is unavailable.

Food Safety Testing

Detection of foodborne pathogens including Salmonella, Listeria monocytogenes, E. coli O157:H7, and Vibrio parahaemolyticus. On-site screening reduces turnaround time from days to under 30 minutes, preventing contaminated products from entering the supply chain.

Animal Disease Surveillance

Early detection of African Swine Fever (ASF), Avian Influenza, and other economically devastating animal diseases. Portable RPA systems enable field-level screening on farms, at borders, and during disease outbreaks, supporting rapid containment strategies.

Environmental Monitoring

Surveillance of water quality, soil microbiomes, and air-borne pathogens. RPA-based biosensors detect trace nucleic acid contamination in environmental samples, supporting regulatory compliance and public health protection.

Antimicrobial Resistance Detection

Rapid identification of antibiotic resistance genes (ARGs) in clinical and environmental isolates. RPA-CRISPR systems distinguish single-nucleotide polymorphisms associated with resistance, informing treatment decisions within hours rather than days.

Zoonotic Disease Monitoring

One Health surveillance of pathogens that transmit between animals and humans. RPA enables simultaneous testing in animal reservoirs and human populations at the human-animal interface, supporting early warning systems for emerging infectious diseases.

PubMed Literature Reviews

Key peer-reviewed publications advancing our understanding of RPA technology, enzyme engineering, and clinical applications

Review · Foundational

Recombinase Polymerase Amplification: Basics, Applications and Recent Advances

Lobato I, O'Sullivan CK Trends in Analytical Chemistry, 2018

A comprehensive review of the RPA platform covering the fundamental principles of recombinase-mediated amplification, primer and probe design guidelines, operational temperature range (22–42°C), and the breadth of detection strategies including fluorescence, lateral flow, and solid-phase formats. The authors highlight how RPA achieves amplification of as few as 1–10 DNA copies within 20 minutes, and discuss integration with microfluidic platforms for automated point-of-care diagnostics.

Key Findings
  • RPA functions across a broad temperature range (22–42°C) without precision thermal control
  • Sensitivity of 1–10 copies per reaction within 20 minutes
  • Multiple detection modalities: real-time fluorescence, lateral flow, and solid-phase ELISA-like formats
  • Microfluidic integration enables fully automated sample-to-answer workflows
Relevance to Dangkang Bio: This review validates the core design principles underlying our RPA enzyme kit — optimised UvsX, Bsu polymerase, and gp32 operating at 37–42°C with molecular crowding reagents for maximum efficiency.

Application · LFA

Application of Recombinase Polymerase Amplification with Lateral Flow Assay to Pathogen Point-of-Care Diagnosis

Zhao Y, Wei Y, Ye C, et al. Frontiers in Cellular and Infection Microbiology, 2024

This article systematically evaluates the RPA-LFA combined detection platform for point-of-care pathogen diagnosis. The lateral flow assay enables visual readout within minutes after RPA amplification, eliminating the need for specialised instrumentation. The authors demonstrate that this integrated approach is particularly suited to resource-limited settings where conventional PCR and real-time fluorescence detection are impractical.

Key Findings
  • RPA-LFA delivers visual results within minutes, requiring no electronic reader
  • Minimal training needed — ideal for field operators and community health workers
  • Cost-effective alternative to PCR for high-volume screening programmes
  • Validated across multiple pathogen targets with consistently high sensitivity and specificity

Review · CRISPR-RPA

Recent Advances in Rapid Multiplex Detection of Nucleic Acid Markers Using RPA and CRISPR-Cas

Li X, Huang Y, Zhang X, et al. Frontiers in Microbiology, 2026

This review covers the rapidly evolving field of RPA-CRISPR combined detection systems operating at isothermal conditions (37–42°C). The authors categorise multiplexing strategies into dual-tube methods, spatially separated single-tube approaches, and homogeneous single-tube systems. Applications span foodborne pathogen screening, antimicrobial resistance gene analysis, and multi-target clinical diagnostics, all achieving attomolar-level sensitivity.

Key Findings
  • RPA-CRISPR systems achieve attomolar sensitivity at isothermal 37–42°C conditions
  • Three multiplexing architectures: dual-tube, spatial single-tube, and homogeneous single-tube
  • Applicable to foodborne pathogens, antibiotic resistance genes, and clinical panels
  • Single-base discrimination enables differentiation of closely related pathogen strains

Deep Learning-Enhanced Hand-Driven Microfluidic Chip for Multiplexed Nucleic Acid Detection Based on RPA/CRISPR

Landmark · 2006

DNA Detection Using Recombination Proteins

Piepenburg O, Williams CH, Stemple DL, Armes NA PLoS Biology, 2006

The foundational publication that first demonstrated recombinase polymerase amplification as a viable method for isothermal DNA detection. The authors proved that the combination of T4 UvsX recombinase, T4 Gene 32 protein, and Bsu DNA polymerase large fragment can amplify target DNA at 37–42°C within 20 minutes. This landmark paper established the three-enzyme RPA system that remains the basis of all modern RPA assays and commercial kits.

Key Findings
  • First demonstration of RPA using UvsX + gp32 + Bsu polymerase large fragment
  • Successful amplification at constant 37–42°C — no thermal cycling required
  • Results in under 20 minutes with high specificity
  • Established the enzyme triad that defines all subsequent RPA technology
